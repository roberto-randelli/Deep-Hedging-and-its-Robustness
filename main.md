# Introduction

## Hedging

The problem of hedging contingent claims lies at the heart of modern
mathematical finance. A contingent claim is a financial contract whose
payoff depends on the future realisation of some underlying asset or
portfolio of assets. The canonical example being a vanilla European call
option, which grants its holder the right to purchase a stock at a fixed
strike price at maturity. From the perspective of a financial
institution that has bought or sold such a contract, the key question is
how to manage the resulting risk associated with the position: that is,
how to construct and dynamically rebalance a portfolio of liquid
instruments so that its terminal value offsets, as closely as possible,
the obligation to the counterparty.

The theoretical foundation for this problem was laid by Black and
Scholes [@black1973pricing] and, independently, Merton
[@merton1973theory], who showed that in a frictionless, complete market
driven by geometric Brownian motion, a contingent claim can be
replicated exactly by a self-financing trading strategy. In this
idealised setting, the no-arbitrage price of the claim coincides with
the cost of its replicating portfolio. This is sometimes referred to as
the pricing-hedging duality [@follmer2016stochastic]. The associated
hedging strategy, known as delta hedging, requires holding at each
instant a position in the underlying equal to the sensitivity of the
option price to changes in the asset price.

In practice, however, the assumptions underpinning the Black-Scholes
framework are systematically violated. Real markets are incomplete and
subject to frictions: transaction costs arise from bid-ask spreads and
brokerage fees, liquidity constraints limit the size and timing of
trades, and portfolios can only be rebalanced at discrete intervals
rather than continuously. As [@buehler2019deep] note, the hedging
instruments available to a practitioner typically include not only
primary assets such as equities, but also liquid options whose
availability may itself be time-dependent. Under these conditions,
perfect replication is generally impossible and the choice of hedging
strategy becomes an optimisation problem, requiring the specification of
a loss criterion that reflects the agent's risk preferences.

Even more difficulty comes from model risk: in practice, the true
dynamics of asset prices are unknown, and any hedging strategy
calibrated to a particular stochastic model will be exposed to the
consequences of model misspecification. As [@he2025] observe, the
standard deep hedging framework assumes that training and deployment
occur under the same probability measure, an assumption that is rarely
satisfied in real markets. Addressing this fragility, and constructing
hedging strategies that remain robust under distributional uncertainty,
is therefore practically very important.

# Background {#ch:background}

## Discrete time markets

Throughout this paper, as in the Deep Hedging framework
[@buehler2019deep], we will be working in a discrete-time financial
market with finite time horizon $T$ and trading dates
$0 = t_0 < t_1 < ... < t_n = T$ and a set of $r$ tradable assets. By
fixing a finite probability space $\Omega$ and a probability measure
$\mathbb{P}$ such that $\mathbb{P}[\{\omega_i\}] > 0$ for all
$\omega_i \in \Omega$, we can define as $\chi$ the set of all random
variables of real value on $\Omega$.

We denote as
$\bm{S} = \{S_1,...,S_{(T+1)}\} \in \mathbb{R}^{r \times (T+1)}$ the
mid-price trajectories of the assets. The price process $\bm{S}$ is
adapted to the filtration $\mathcal{F} = (\mathcal{F}_t)_{t=0}^{T}$
generated by the information process
$\mathcal{I} = (\mathcal{I}_t)_{t=0}^{T}$, where each
$I_t \in \mathbb{R}^d$ captures market relevant features at time $t$,
such as asset prices, volatility, risk limits, and trading signals. The
trajectory $\mathcal{I}$ is drawn from a known distribution $\mu$, which
may represent either a stochastic model or a uniform distribution over
simulated or historical scenarios.

No assumptions are made about the existence of an EMM (equivalent market
measure) or about the availability of hedging instruments at any point
in time. The objective is to hedge a portfolio of derivatives $Z$ that
is $\mathcal{F}_T$ measurable. The maturity $T$ is the maximum maturity
of any instrument.

To hedge $Z$, trading is conducted in the assets $S$ via an
$\mathbb{R}^r$, $\mathcal{F}$-adapted process
$\delta = (\delta_k)_{k=0,\ldots,n-1}$, where
$\delta_k = (\delta_k^1, \ldots, \delta_k^r).$ Here, $\delta_k^i$
represents the position held in the $i$-th asset at time $t_k$. Since
the strategy is self-financing, an initial capital $p_0$ may be injected
at time $t_0$. The terminal profit and loss is then given by
$$\mathrm{PnL}(Z, p_0, \delta) = -Z + p_0 + (\delta \cdot S)_T - C_T(\delta),$$
where
$$(\delta \cdot S)_T = \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} \delta_k \bigl(S_{k+1} - S_k\bigr)$$
denotes the cumulative gains and losses from trading, and
$$C_T(\delta) = \sum_{k=0}^{n} c_k(\delta_{k+1} - \delta_k)$$ represents
the total transaction costs incurred up to maturity.

## Convex Risk Measures {#sec:background_oce}

Theoretically, if we have a complete market, with continuous time
trading, no transaction costs, and unconstrained hedging, we know that
for any contingent claim $Z$ there exists a unique replication strategy
$\delta$ and a fair price $p_0 \in \mathbb{R}$ such that
$-Z + p_0 + (\delta \cdot S)_T - C_T(\delta) = 0$ holds
$\mathbb{P}$-a.s. Unfortunately, this is not our current setting or the
real world setting.

In fact, the market may be incomplete and have frictions, which
necessitates the specification of an optimality criterion in order to
determine an appropriate price for a contingent claim. Such a price can
be interpreted as the minimal capital that must be added to a position
so that, when hedged optimally, the resulting position becomes
acceptable according to the agent's preferences. This notion of
acceptability is formalised through convex risk measures. These have
been studied in [@convexRiskPricing2006] and [@ilhan2009optimal] for
example.

::: definition
**Definition 2.1**. *Let $X, X_1, X_2 \in \mathcal{X}$ be financial
positions. A function $\rho : \mathcal{X} \to \mathbb{R}$ is called a
*convex risk measure* if it satisfies:*

1.  ***Monotonicity:** If $X_1 \geq X_2$, then
    $\rho(X_1) \leq \rho(X_2)$.*

2.  ***Convexity:** For any $\alpha \in [0,1]$,
    $$\rho(\alpha X_1 + (1-\alpha)X_2) \leq \alpha \rho(X_1) + (1-\alpha)\rho(X_2).$$*

3.  ***Cash invariance:** For all $c \in \mathbb{R}$,
    $$\rho(X + c) = \rho(X) - c.$$*
:::

Given a convex risk measure $\rho$, we consider the optimisation problem
$$\pi(X) = \inf_{\delta \in \mathcal{H}} \rho\big(X + (\delta \cdot S)_T - C_T(\delta)\big),$$
where $\mathcal{H}$ denotes the set of admissible trading strategies
subject to market constraints.

::: proposition
**Proposition 2.1** (see Prop. 3.2, p. 7 [@buehler2019deep]). *The
functional $\pi$ is monotone decreasing and cash-invariant. Moreover, if
$C_T$ and $\mathcal{H}$ are convex, then $\pi$ defines a convex risk
measure.*
:::

An optimal hedging strategy is any $\delta \in \mathcal{H}$ that
minimises the above expression. The quantity $\pi(X)$ may be interpreted
as the minimal capital required to render a position acceptable under
$\rho$. However, this definition may lead to inconsistencies, for
instance allowing strictly positive values even in the absence of
liabilities due to positive expected returns.

To address this, we instead consider the *indifference price* $p(Z)$,
defined by $$\pi(-Z + p(Z)) = \pi(0).$$ Using cash invariance, this can
be rewritten as $$p(Z) = \pi(-Z) - \pi(0).$$

In a frictionless setting, this definition coincides with the price of a
replicating portfolio whenever such a portfolio exists.

We now focus on a particular class of convex risk measures known as
*Optimised Certainty Equivalents (OCE)*.

::: definition
**Definition 2.2**. *Let $l : \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ be a
continuous, non-decreasing, and convex loss function. The optimised
certainty equivalent is defined by
$$\rho(X) = \inf_{w \in \mathbb{R}} \left\{ w + \mathbb{E}\big[l(-X - w)\big] \right\}.$$*
:::

Two important special cases are:

1.  For $\lambda > 0$, choosing
    $$l(x) = \exp(\lambda x) - \frac{1 + \log(x)}{\lambda}$$ yields the
    *entropic risk measure*
    $$\rho(X) = \frac{1}{\lambda} \log \mathbb{E}[\exp(-\lambda X)].$$

2.  Setting
    $$l(x) = \frac{1}{1-\alpha} \max(x,0), \quad \alpha \in (0,1),$$
    leads to the *average value at risk* (also known as expected
    shortfall, or CVar).

Consider now the exponential utility function
$U(x) = -\exp(-\lambda x)$. The following result establishes its
connection to the entropic risk measure.

::: proposition
**Proposition 2.2** (see Lemma 3.5, p. 10 [@buehler2019deep]). *Let
$q(Z)$ solve
$$\sup_{\delta \in \mathcal{H}} \mathbb{E}\big[U(q(Z) - Z + (\delta \cdot S)_T - C_T(\delta))\big] 
=
\sup_{\delta \in \mathcal{H}} \mathbb{E}\big[U((\delta \cdot S)_T - C_T(\delta))\big].$$
Then $q(Z) = p(Z)$.*
:::

This specification of preferences is widely used in modern financial
modelling, particularly in the presence of market frictions.

## Neural networks

We begin by recalling the definition of a feedforward neural network,
which constitutes the fundamental component of our model.

::: definition
**Definition 2.3**. *Let $L \in \mathbb{N}$,
$N_0, \ldots, N_L \in \mathbb{N}$, and let
$\sigma_i : \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ for $i = 1, \ldots, L$. Let
$A_i : \mathbb{R}^{N_{i-1}} \to \mathbb{R}^{N_i}$ be affine functions. A
mapping $F : \mathbb{R}^{N_0} \to \mathbb{R}^{N_L}$ defined by
$$F(x) = F_L \circ \cdots \circ F_1(x),$$ where
$$F_i = \sigma_i \circ A_i, \quad i = 1, \ldots, L,$$ is called a neural
network.*
:::

The activation functions $\sigma_i$ are applied componentwise. The
integer $L$ denotes the number of layers, $N_1, \ldots, N_{L-1}$ specify
the sizes of the hidden layers, while $N_0$ and $N_L$ represent the
input and output dimensions, respectively.

We denote by $\mathcal{NN}^{\Sigma}_{\infty, d_0, d_1}$ the class of all
neural networks mapping $\mathbb{R}^{d_0}$ to $\mathbb{R}^{d_1}$, where
$\Sigma$ specifies the collection of activation functions used in each
layer. The following theorem highlights the expressive power of neural
networks.

::: theorem
**Theorem 2.1** (Universal approximation, see [@hornik1991approximation]
). *Suppose that each element of $\sigma$ is bounded and non-constant.
Then the following statements hold:*

1.  *For any finite measure $\mu$ on
    $(\mathbb{R}^{d_0}, \mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R}^{d_0}))$ and
    $1 \leq p < \infty$, the set
    $\mathcal{NN}^{\Sigma}_{\infty, d_0, d_1}$ is dense in
    $L^p(\mathbb{R}^{d_0}, \mu)$.*

2.  *If, in addition, every element of $\Sigma$ is continuously
    differentiable on $\mathbb{R}$, then
    $\mathcal{NN}^{\Sigma}_{\infty, d_0, d_1}$ is dense in
    $C(\mathbb{R}^{d_0})$ with respect to the topology of uniform
    convergence on compact sets.*
:::

For computational purposes, we consider a sequence of subsets of
$\mathcal{NN}^{\Sigma}_{\infty, d_0, d_1}$, denoted by
$\{\mathcal{NN}^{\Sigma}_{M, d_0, d_1}\}_{M \in \mathbb{N}}$, satisfying
the following properties:

1.  $\mathcal{NN}^{\Sigma}_{M, d_0, d_1} \subset \mathcal{NN}^{\Sigma}_{M+1, d_0, d_1}$
    for all $M \in \mathbb{N}$,

2.  $\bigcup_{M \in \mathbb{N}} \mathcal{NN}^{\Sigma}_{M, d_0, d_1} = \mathcal{NN}^{\Sigma}_{\infty, d_0, d_1}$,

3.  For any $M \in \mathbb{N}$,
    $\mathcal{NN}^{\Sigma}_{M, d_0, d_1} = \{ F^\theta : \theta \in \Theta_{M, d_0, d_1} \}$,
    where $\Theta_{M, d_0, d_1} \subset \mathbb{R}^k$ for some
    $k \in \mathbb{N}$.

One may interpret the sequence defined above as the collection of neural
networks with fixed architecture, parametrised by a vector $\theta$,
whose dimension depends on $M$.

## Deep Hedging Framework

In the deep hedging framework, each trading strategy at time $t$,
$\delta_t \in \mathbb{R}^r$, which represents the fraction of portfolio
wealth in the $r$ assets at time $t$, is determined by a neural network
with parameters $\theta = (\theta_1, ..., \theta_T)$ and takes the
history of available information up to time $t$ as input,
$\delta_t = f_{\theta_t}(I_1,...,I_t)$ where $f_{\theta}$ is a function
parametrised by network weights $\theta_t \in \mathbb{R}^n$. So, the
strategy depends on the network parameters $\theta$ and the information
process $\mathcal{I}$.

Thus, the $PnL$ formula becomes:
$$PnL(\theta,\mathcal{I}) = p_0 + -Z + (\delta(\theta,\mathcal{I}) \cdot S)_T - C_T(\delta(\theta,\mathcal{I}))$$
for a given price $p_0 \geq 0$.

The objective is thus to minimize the $PnL(\theta,\mathcal{I})$ outcome
of the hedging strategy under a convex risk measure, so, solve
min$_{\theta in \Theta}\rho[PnL(\theta,\mathcal{I})]$.

This is all possible because of the following proposition:

::: theorem
**Theorem 2.2** (see Prop. 4.9, p. 13 [@buehler2019deep]). *Let
$\mathcal{H}_M$ denote the subspace of all strategies $\delta$ that can
be obtained with the use of networks belonging to
$\mathcal{NN}^{\Sigma}_{M,d_0,d_1}$. Then $$\lim_{M \to \infty} \pi^M(X)
=
\lim_{M \to \infty}
\inf_{\delta \in \mathcal{H}_M}
\rho\bigl(X + (\delta \cdot S)_T - C_T(\delta)\bigr)
=
\pi(X).$$*
:::

This result follows from the universal approximation theorem, which
ensures that neural networks can approximate any measurable function,
and since optimal hedging strategies can be expressed as functions of
the available information, it follows that neural networks are capable
of approximating the optimal strategy arbitrarily well.

## Model Uncertainty and Robustness

The deep hedging framework described above assumes that $\mathcal{I}$ is
drawn from a fixed distribution $\mu$. This is not true in practice. As
demonstrated in [@he2025], strategies that perform well in $\mu$ could
perform badly even against little shift in distribution. Thus, the deep
hedging framework is subject to model risk as true data can differ from
the training model. This motivates the study of robust hedging methods
that withstand such uncertainty.

Naturally, we would like to replace the measure $\mu$ with a
neighborhood of alternatives and to do this we need a notion of distance
between probability measures; the Wasserstein distance.

## The Wasserstein Distance

Let $S \subseteq \mathbb{R}^d$ be a closed set and let $\mathcal{P}(S)$
denote the set of all Borel probability measures on $S$. Equip
$\mathbb{R}^d$ with a norm $\|\cdot\|$ and let $\|\cdot\|_*$ denote its
dual norm, i.e. $\|y\|_* = \sup\{\langle x, y\rangle : \|x\| \leq
1\}$.

::: definition
**Definition 2.4** (Wasserstein distance, see [@bartl2021sensitivity]).
*For $p \geq 1$ and $\mu, \nu \in \mathcal{P}(S)$, the *$p$-Wasserstein
distance* between $\mu$ and $\nu$ is defined as
$$W_p(\mu, \nu) = \left( \inf_{\pi \in \mathrm{Cpl}(\mu,\nu)} \int_{S \times S} \|x - y\|_*^p
\, \pi(\mathrm{d}x, \mathrm{d}y) \right)^{1/p},$$ where
$\mathrm{Cpl}(\mu, \nu)$ is the set of all probability measures $\pi$ on
$S \times S$ whose first marginal is $\mu$ and second marginal is
$\nu$.*
:::

Intuitively, $W_p(\mu, \nu)$ measures the minimum cost of transporting
mass from $\mu$ to $\nu$, where the cost of moving a unit of mass from
$x$ to $y$ is $\|x-y\|_*^p$. Most importantly, the Wasserstein distance
has a key property for this paper. Unlike other distances, like total
variation for instance, which becomes infinite when two distributions
don't share the same support, the Wasserstein distance two measures can
be close in Wasserstein distance even if they have different
supports [@he2025; @bai2023wasserstein].

### Wasserstein Ball {#wasserstein-ball .unnumbered}

::: definition
**Definition 2.5** (Wasserstein ball, see [@bartl2021sensitivity]). *The
*Wasserstein ball* of radius $\delta \geq 0$ centred at
$\mu \in \mathcal{P}(S)$ is defined as
$$\mathcal{B}_\delta(\mu) = \{ \nu \in \mathcal{P}(S) : W_p(\mu, \nu) \leq \delta \}.$$*
:::

The ball $\mathcal{B}_\delta(\mu)$ collects all probability measures
that can be reached from the reference model $\mu$ at a transport cost
of at most $\delta$. When $\delta = 0$ the ball collapses to the
singleton $\{\mu\}$, recovering the original distribution. The $\delta$
parameter is very important as it regulates how the true distribution is
allowed to diviate from the original $\mu$.

A useful probabilistic representation of the worst-case expectation over
a Wasserstein ball is available [@bartl2021sensitivity]: given a random
variable $X \sim \mu$, one has
$$\sup_{\nu \in \mathcal{B}_\delta(\mu)} \int_S f(x)\, \nu(\mathrm{d}x)
= \sup \, \mathbb{E}[f(X + Z)],$$ where the supremum on the right is
taken over all perturbations $Z$ satisfying $X + Z \in S$ almost surely
and $\mathbb{E}[\|Z\|_*^p] \leq \delta^p$.

## Distributionally Robust Optimisation {#sec:background_dro}

### The DRO Problem

Distributionally robust optimisation (DRO) provides a framework for
decision making under model uncertainty. The main idea is to replace
expectation under the measure $\mu$ with the worst-case expectation an
ambiguity set of plausible measures.

Formally, the DRO problem takes the form of a two-player minimax game:
$$\inf_{\theta \in \Theta} \sup_{\eta \in \mathcal{B}_\delta(\mu)} \mathbb{E}_{x \sim \eta}
[l(\theta; x)],$$ where $\theta \in \Theta$ is the decision variable
(representing the first, minimising player), $l(\theta; x)$ is a loss
function parametrised by $\theta$ and evaluated at an input $x$, and the
adversarial second player selects the worst distribution $\eta$ from the
ambiguity set $\mathcal{B}_\delta(\mu)$ [@he2025]. When $\delta = 0$ the
ambiguity set degenerates to $\{\mu\}$ and the DRO problem reduces to
the standard stochastic optimisation problem
$\inf_{\theta \in \Theta} \mathbb{E}_{x \sim \mu}[l(\theta; x)]$.

When the ambiguity set is specified as a Wasserstein ball, as defined
above, the resulting framework is known as Wasserstein Distributionally
Robust Optimisation (W-DRO), and is the approach we adopt in this work
and that is used in [@he2025]. As mentioned above, W-DRO is used as it
can also capture perturbations across different supports and
following [@he2025], we adopt the $W_\infty$ threat model, equipping the
path space with the sup-norm $d(S, \hat{S}) = \max_t |S_t - \hat{S}_t|$,
where $S_t$ is the price path.

In the deep hedging context, the loss function is the convex risk
measure applied to the hedging PnL,
i.e. $l(\theta; \mathcal{I}) = \rho[\mathrm{PnL}(\theta, \mathcal{I})]$,
and the decision variable $\theta$ represents the parameters of the
neural network. The W-DRO problem therefore seeks network parameters
that minimise the worst-case hedging loss over all plausible
data-generating processes within a Wasserstein neighbourhood of the
reference model $\mu$.

### Tractability via First-Order Sensitivity

Even if there is this theoretical appeal, W-DRO is computationally
challenging. The inner supremum is an optimisation over the
infinite-dimensional space of probability measures, which is generally
intractable [@he2025; @gonon2023robustness].

The approach used in [@he2025], which was pioneered
in [@bartl2021sensitivity], computes the first-order approximation to
the W-DRO value in the size of the uncertainty radius $\delta$. The key
result can be stated as follows.

::: {#thm:fo_sensitivity .theorem}
**Theorem 2.3** (First-order W-DRO sensitivity, adapted from Theorem 2.2
in [@bartl2021sensitivity] and Theorem 4.1 in [@bai2023wasserstein]).
*Let
$V(\delta) = \sup_{\nu \in \mathcal{B}_\delta(\mu)} \mathbb{E}_{x \sim \nu}[l(\theta; x)]$
denote the worst-case loss at budget $\delta$. Suppose the map
$x \mapsto l(\theta; x)$ is Lipschitz. Then the following first-order
expansion holds:
$$V(\delta) = V(0) + \delta \cdot \Upsilon + o(\delta),$$ where
$$\Upsilon = \left( \mathbb{E}_\mu \bigl[ \| \nabla_x l(\theta; x) \|_*^q \bigr] \right)^{1/q},$$
with $q$ the conjugate exponent to $p$ (i.e. $1/p + 1/q = 1$). Moreover,
the worst-case distribution $\nu_\delta$ achieving
$V(\delta) + o(\delta)$ is the pushforward of $\mu$ under the map
$x \mapsto x + \delta \, T(x)$, where $T$ is the direction of steepest
ascent of the loss.*
:::

In particular, we can see $\Upsilon$ as the model sentivity of the
optimisarion problem. $\Upsilon$ measures how the loss grows with
$\delta$. Also, $\Upsilon$ can be computed using first order information
like gradients of the loss with respect to the inputs, making it perfect
to gradient-based optimisation. This first-order approach was used
in [@bartl2021sensitivity] to derive new non-parametric financial
sensitivities and forms the theoretical backbone of the adversarial
training methods
in [@bai2023wasserstein; @gonon2023robustness; @he2025].

## Adversarial Training for Deep Hedging

### Adversarial Attacks

The first results on neural network vulnerability were identified in
[-@goodfellow2014explaining]. They looked at what effect small
perturbations would have on the input in image classification. In that
context, an attacker modifies individual input images by an
imperceptible amount to cause misclassification. In deep hedging, this
translates to the problem of analysing if a trained hedging strategy may
perform poorly when the market dynamics deviate, even slightly, from the
model used during training.

There are two types of adversarial attacks. The pointwise attack which
changes each path within a $\delta$-ball centred at the input to
maximize the corresponding loss,
$\max_{\hat{x}\in\mathbb{R}^d}l(\theta;\hat{x})$ with
$d(\hat{x},x)\le \delta$. The two most important algorithms are the Fast
Gradient Sign Method (FGSM) of Goodfellow et al.
[@goodfellow2014explaining] and the PGD attack of Madry et al
[@madry2018towards].

The distributional attack instead changes the entire data-generating
distribution, selecting the worst measure
$\nu \in \mathcal{B}_\delta(\mu)$ in the Wasserstein ball which
maximizes the expected loss of the model:
$V_\theta(\delta)=\max_{\nu\in B_\delta(\mu)}\mathbb{E}_{x\sim\nu}[l(\theta;x)]$.
The distributional threat model is strictly stronger than the pointwise
one (for $1 \leq p < \infty$) because the attacker is free to
concentrate its budget on the most damaging scenarios while leaving
others unchanged [@bai2023wasserstein].

The first-order sensitivity result
(Theorem [2.3](#thm:fo_sensitivity){reference-type="ref"
reference="thm:fo_sensitivity"}) immediately yields a practical attack
algorithm: given the current network parameters $\theta$, the worst-case
perturbation of $x$ in the direction of the loss gradient is
$$x \mapsto x + \delta \cdot T(x), \quad T(x) = \Upsilon^{-1} \frac{\nabla_x l(\theta; x)}
{\| \nabla_x l(\theta; x) \|}.$$

### Adversarial Training: W-DRO in Practice

Adversarial training converts the W-DRO minimax problem into a learning
algorithm by alternating between two steps. These two steps are an inner
maximisation that generates adversarially perturbed paths holding
$\theta$ fixed, and then the outer minimisation that updates the network
parameters on the augmented data [@he2025]. The training loss combines
clean and adversarial samples:
$$\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{adv}}(\theta) = \alpha \sum_{n=1}^N l_{\mathrm{DH}}(\theta; X_n) +
\sum_{n=1}^N l_{\mathrm{DH}}(\theta; \hat{X}_n),$$ where $\{X_n\}$ are
draws from the nominal model $\mu$, $\{\hat{X}_n\}$ are the
corresponding adversarially perturbed paths obtained from the W-PGD
attack, and $\alpha \geq 0$ balances the weight given to clean versus
adversarial samples [@he2025]. When $\alpha = 0$ training is purely
adversarial; when $\alpha \to \infty$ it reduces to standard
(non-robust) deep hedging.

Intuitively, by exposing the network to worst-case distributional
perturbations during training, we encourage it to learn strategies that
remain effective across a neighbourhood of plausible models, rather than
strategies that are finely tuned to a single data-generating process.
The adversarially trained network approximately solves
$$\min_{\theta \in \Theta} \sup_{\nu \in \mathcal{B}_\delta(\mu)} \mathbb{E}_\nu
\bigl[ \rho(\mathrm{PnL}(\theta, \mathcal{I})) \bigr],$$

ensuring that the hedging strategy is optimal in the worst-case model
within the Wasserstein ball of radius $\delta$ around the reference
measure.

This framework was first applied to deep hedging in [@he2025], where it
is demonstrated empirically that adversarially trained strategies
substantially outperform classically trained ones on out-of-sample paths
and on entirely different generative models, even with training sets as
small as 5,000 paths. The robustness gains are especially pronounced
during periods of market stress, where distributional shifts are most
severe. These findings provide the core motivation for the methodology
developed in the next section.

# Black - Scholes model {#ch:bs}

We have established the underlying theory in Chapter
[2](#ch:background){reference-type="ref" reference="ch:background"},
which comprehends the deep hedging framework, the convex risk measures
that drive training, and the W-DRO robustness theory. So, we now turn to
numerical validation, beginning with the Black--Scholes (BS) model.

## Background Theory: the Black--Scholes Delta {#sec:bs_benchmark}

Under the risk-neutral dynamics
$$dS_t = \sigma S_t\,dW_t^{Q}, \qquad S_0 = 100,$$ with annualised
volatility $\sigma = 0.2$ and zero risk-free rate, the fair price of a
European call with strike $K=S_0$ and maturity $T$ is given by the
Black--Scholes formula
$$C(t,S_t)= S_t\,\mathcal{N}(d_+) - K\,\mathcal{N}(d_-),$$ where
$\mathcal{N}$ is the standard normal CDF and
$$d_\pm = \frac{\log(S_t/K)\pm\tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2(T-t)}{\sigma\sqrt{T-t}}.$$
The replicating delta at time $t$ is
$\Delta_t^{\mathrm{BS}} = \mathcal{N}(d_+)$: holding
$\Delta_t^{\mathrm{BS}}$ units of the stock at every instant replicates
the payoff perfectly under continuous trading. In discrete time with
$N=30$ daily steps over a horizon of $T = 30/365$ years, a residual
hedging error persists due to the inability to rebalance continuous time
trading. Thus, this discretisation error constitutes the entire risk we
would like to measure.

## Data Generation {#sec:bs_data}

To simulate the BS model we generated $N_{\mathrm{paths}}=100{,}000$
stock price paths using the exact log-normal transition
$$S_{t_{k+1}} = S_{t_k}\exp\!\left(-\tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2\Delta t
               + \sigma\sqrt{\Delta t}\,Z_k\right),
  \qquad Z_k\stackrel{\text{iid}}{\sim}\mathcal{N}(0,1),$$ with
$\Delta t = T/N$ and a fixed random seed.

## Network Architecture {#sec:bs_arch}

Similarly to the methodology presented in [@he2025] and
[@buehler2019deep], we construct a separate network in each of the
$N=30$ time steps, with no weight sharing across time. This avoids the
vanishing-gradient difficulties of recurrence. At step $t_k$ the network
maps $$\log S_{t_k} \;\longmapsto\; \delta_{t_k}\in\mathbb{R},$$ where
$\delta_{t_k}$ is the share position held over $(t_k,t_{k+1}]$. The
architecture is $$\mathrm{BN}(1)
  \to \mathrm{Linear}(1,20)
  \to \mathrm{BN}
  \to \mathrm{ReLU}
  \to \mathrm{Linear}(20,20)
  \to \mathrm{BN}
  \to \mathrm{ReLU}
  \to \mathrm{Linear}(20,1),$$ where $\mathrm{BN}$ denotes batch
normalisation. The two hidden layers of width 20 total roughly 500
trainable parameters per step.

## Training with the CVaR Loss {#sec:bs_cvar_loss}

As established in
Section [2.2](#sec:background_oce){reference-type="ref"
reference="sec:background_oce"}, the deep hedging training objective is
to minimise an OCE risk measure applied to the terminal PnL
$$X(\theta,\omega) = \omega + p_0
    + \sum_{k=0}^{N-1}\delta_{t_k}(S_{t_{k+1}}-S_{t_k})
    - (S_T - K)^+,$$ where $\omega$ is a trainable scalar and $p_0$ is
the Black--Scholes risk-neutral price (held fixed). We use the CVaR
(Expected Shortfall) loss at four confidence levels
$\alpha\in\{0.50, 0.75, 0.95, 0.99\}$. In the implementation we use the
Rockafellar--Uryasev representation $$\mathrm{CVaR}_\alpha(X)
  = \min_{z\in\mathbb{R}}\left\{
      z + \frac{1}{1-\alpha}\,\mathbb{E}[\max(-X-z,\,0)]
    \right\},$$ where $z$ is jointly optimised with the network
parameters [@rockafellar2000cvar]. All four networks are trained with
the Adam optimiser [@kingma2015adam] using a decaying learning rate
schedule $1.0\to 0.1\to 0.01\to 0.001$ over approximately 500 epochs.

# Heston Stochastic Volatility Model {#ch:heston}

The GBM setting of the previous chapter is a standard classical result
but is too vanilla to explain phenomena happening in financial markets.
Thus the Heston model was introduced [@heston1993closed] to capture the
volatility clustering, negative skewness, and heavy tails observed in
equity markets. It is only natural to extend the deep hedging framework
to the Heston stochastic volatility model, which introduces a second
risk factor that cannot be hedged by trading the stock alone.

## Model Specification {#sec:heston_model}

Under the risk-neutral measure $Q$ the Heston model is specified by
$$\begin{align}
  dS_t  &= \sqrt{V_t}\,S_t\,dW_t^{1,Q}, \qquad S_0 = 100, \label{eq:heston_s}\\
  dV_t  &= \kappa(\theta-V_t)\,dt + \xi\sqrt{V_t}\,dW_t^{2,Q}, \qquad V_0 = 0.04,
  \label{eq:heston_v}
\end{align}$$ where $W^{1,Q}$ and $W^{2,Q}$ are correlated Brownian
motions with $\mathrm{d}\langle W^1,W^2\rangle_t = \rho\,\mathrm{d}t$.
We set $\kappa=1$, $\theta=0.04$, $\xi=2$, and $\rho=-0.7$, matching the
parameters used in [@buehler2019deep] and [@he2025]. These values imply
a long-run annualised volatility of $\sqrt{\theta}=0.2$ which is
consistent with the GBM calibration.

## Completing the Market with a Variance Swap {#sec:heston_varswap}

The Heston model has two sources of randomness, thus, if you trade with
only the stock and the money market account, you can only account for
one source of randomness. Therefore, the Heston market with only the
stock as hedging instrument is incomplete. So, we introduce an idealised
variance swap whose fair value at time $t$ is
$$S_t^{(2)} := \mathbb{E}^Q\!\left[\int_0^T V_u\,\mathrm{d}u\,\Big|\,\mathcal{F}_t\right].$$
to complete the market, following what was done in [@buehler2019deep].

Using the Fubini theorem and the closed form expectation of the CIR
process, this simplifies to $$S_t^{(2)} = \int_0^t V_u\,\mathrm{d}u
             + \frac{V_t - \theta}{\kappa}(1-e^{-\kappa(T-t)})
             + \theta(T-t),$$ which is an explicit and computable
function of $(t, V_t)$ and the accumulated realised variance
$\int_0^t V_u\,\mathrm{d}u$.

So, during simulation, we can track the latter. In continuous time, this
would be enough to replicate any European option perfectly, according to
the delta strategy of [@buehler2019deep], equation (5.6).

##### Gradient rescaling.

A critical numerical detail is that the magnitudes of $S^{(1)}$
(order $10^2$) and $S^{(2)}$ (order $10^{-2}$ to $10^{-1}$ for our
parameters) differ by several orders of magnitude. Without rescaling,
gradients with respect to the variance-swap position $\delta^{(2)}$ are
roughly three orders of magnitude smaller than those for $\delta^{(1)}$,
causing the network to overlook the variance swap entirely during
training. We therefore rescale $S^{(2)}$ by its cross-sectional mean at
$t=0$, estimated from the training sample:
$$\widetilde{S}_t^{(2)} = \frac{S_t^{(2)}}{\overline{S_0^{(2)}}}.$$

## Data Generation: Broadie-Kaya Exact Scheme {#sec:heston_sim}

We know that the Euler--Maruyama discretisation of the Heston SDE
introduces a bias in the variance process. In fact, this is particularly
visible when the Feller condition is violated and $V_t$ is near zero. We
therefore simulate using the Broadie--Kaya exact scheme
[@broadie2006exact], which samples the joint law of
$(V_{t_{k+1}},\int_{t_k}^{t_{k+1}} V_u\,\mathrm{d}u)$ exactly via
noncentral chi-squared random variables and recovers $S_{t_{k+1}}$ by
conditioning on the integrated variance. This produces unbiased paths at
the expense of greater computational cost, and it is the
methodologically correct choice given the Feller violation. We generate
$100{,}000$ paths of $N=30$ steps, yielding tensors
$(S^{(1)}, V, \widetilde{S}^{(2)})$ for training and evaluation.

## Network Architecture {#sec:heston_arch}

The Heston network mirrors the GBM architecture of
Section [3.3](#sec:bs_arch){reference-type="ref"
reference="sec:bs_arch"} but takes two inputs and produces two outputs
at each step. The information process is
$I_{t_k} = (\log S_{t_k}^{(1)}, V_{t_k})$, and the network maps
$$(\log S_{t_k}^{(1)},\, V_{t_k})
  \;\longmapsto\;
  (\delta_{t_k}^{(1)},\, \delta_{t_k}^{(2)}),$$ where $\delta^{(1)}$ and
$\delta^{(2)}$ are the positions in the stock and the rescaled variance
swap respectively. The per-step architecture is $$\mathrm{BN}(2)
  \to \mathrm{Linear}(2,20)
  \to \mathrm{BN}
  \to \mathrm{ReLU}
  \to \mathrm{Linear}(20,20)
  \to \mathrm{BN}
  \to \mathrm{ReLU}
  \to \mathrm{Linear}(20,2).$$ The terminal PnL accumulates
contributions from both instruments: $$X(\theta)
  = \omega + p_0
    + \sum_{k=0}^{N-1}\!\Bigl[
        \delta_{t_k}^{(1)}\Delta S_{t_{k+1}}^{(1)}
      + \delta_{t_k}^{(2)}\Delta\widetilde{S}_{t_{k+1}}^{(2)}
    \Bigr]
    - (S_T^{(1)}-K)^+.$$ The same four CVaR levels are used as in the
GBM case, with 700--1500 epochs of Adam training. In this case, more
epochs are necessary due to the state space which has two dimensions and
the more complex loss surface.

## Analytical Benchmark: Heston Delta {#sec:heston_delta}

One the theoretical side, we used the same implementation as in
[@buehler2019deep] for the Heston Delta. The call price
$u(t, S_t^{(1)}, V_t)$ is evaluated via the *little Heston trap*
characteristic function [@albrecher2007little]. This is then followed by
numerical Fourier inversion with $n_u = 128$ quadrature points over
$u \in (0, 100]$ using the trapezoidal rule. The partial derivatives are
then obtained by symmetric central differences: $$\delta_{t_k}^{(1)}
  = \frac{u(t_k,\, S_{t_k}^{(1)} + \varepsilon_S,\, V_{t_k})
        - u(t_k,\, S_{t_k}^{(1)} - \varepsilon_S,\, V_{t_k})}
         {2\varepsilon_S},$$ $$\delta_{t_k}^{(2)}
  = \frac{1}{\partial_v L(t_k, V_{t_k})}
    \cdot
    \frac{u(t_k,\, S_{t_k}^{(1)},\, V_{t_k} + \varepsilon_V)
        - u(t_k,\, S_{t_k}^{(1)},\, V_{t_k} - \varepsilon_V)}
         {2\varepsilon_V},$$ where $\varepsilon_S = 10^{-3}$,
$\varepsilon_V = 10^{-5}$, and
$\partial_v L(t,v) = (1 - e^{-\kappa(T-t)})/\kappa$. This is
mathematically equivalent to the QuantLib implementation used in
[@buehler2019deep] but it avoids an external library dependency.

# Distributional Adversarial Attacks {#ch:attacks}

In Chapters [3](#ch:bs){reference-type="ref" reference="ch:bs"},
[4](#ch:heston){reference-type="ref" reference="ch:heston"} we verified
that deep learning networks can execute close to optimal hedging
strategies when the training and testing distributions are exactly
known. We now turn to the central robustness question: how does a
classically trained network perform when the true market distribution
deviates from the nominal model $\mu$ used during training? Using the
W-DRO framework developed in
Section [2.7](#sec:background_dro){reference-type="ref"
reference="sec:background_dro"}, we quantify this robustness gap through
distributional adversarial attacks.

## The Adversarial Attack Problem {#sec:attack_setup}

Let $\theta$ be the parameters of a trained deep hedging network and let
$$l_{\mathrm{DH}}(\theta; S_n)
  = \mathrm{CVaR}_\alpha\bigl[-\mathrm{PnL}(\theta, S_n)\bigr]$$ be the
deep hedging loss evaluated on a price path $S_n$. Thus, the
distributional adversarial attack problem is defined as: given $N$
training paths $\{S_n\}_{n=1}^N$ drawn from the nominal model $\mu$,
$$\begin{equation}
  \max_{\hat{S}_1,\ldots,\hat{S}_N}
      \frac{1}{N}\sum_{n=1}^N l_{\mathrm{DH}}(\theta;\hat{S}_n)
  \quad\text{subject to}\quad
  \left(\frac{1}{N}\sum_{n=1}^N d(S_n,\hat{S}_n)^p\right)^{1/p}\leq\delta,
  \label{eq:attack_prob}
\end{equation}$$ where $\delta > 0$ is the attack budget and $d$ is a
transport cost that measures how far the perturbed path $\hat{S}_n$ has
moved from the original path $S_n$.

As done in [@he2025], we work in the $L^\infty$ path space and define
$$\begin{equation}
  d(S_n, \hat{S}_n)
  = \|S_n - \hat{S}_n\|_\infty
  = \max_{t=0,\ldots,N} |S_{n,t} - \hat{S}_{n,t}|.
  \label{eq:linf_dist}
\end{equation}$$

The initial stock price $S_0$ is never changed. This is observable and
fixed at the time of the option sale. Also, all prices are clamped below
at $0.01$ to prevent non-positive values.

## From W-DRO Sensitivity to a Practical Attack {#sec:attack_theory}

Unfortunately,
Problem [\[eq:attack_prob\]](#eq:attack_prob){reference-type="eqref"
reference="eq:attack_prob"} is computationally intractable, as it is an
optimisation over the infinite dimensional space of empirical measures.
Thus, we reduce the problem to a finite dimensional gradient
computation. This is the key contribution of [@he2025], building on
Theorem [2.3](#thm:fo_sensitivity){reference-type="ref"
reference="thm:fo_sensitivity"} from
Chapter [2](#ch:background){reference-type="ref"
reference="ch:background"}.

By the first-order W-DRO sensitivity result, the worst-case loss admits
the expansion
$$V_\theta(\delta) = V_\theta(0) + \delta \cdot \Upsilon + o(\delta),$$
where the *model sensitivity* $\Upsilon$ is $$\begin{equation}
  \Upsilon
  = \left(\frac{1}{N}\sum_{n=1}^N
          \bigl\|\nabla_{S_n} l_{\mathrm{DH}}(\theta; S_n)\bigr\|_*^q
    \right)^{1/q},
  \label{eq:upsilon}
\end{equation}$$ with $q = p/(p-1)$ the Hölder conjugate of $p$. For the
$L^\infty / L^1$ dual pair used here, $q = 1$ and
$\|\cdot\|_* = \|\cdot\|_1$, so $$\Upsilon
  = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{n=1}^N
    \bigl\|\nabla_{S_n} l_{\mathrm{DH}}(\theta; S_n)\bigr\|_1.$$ The
worst-case perturbation that achieves
$V_\theta(0) + \delta\Upsilon + o(\delta)$ moves each path in the
direction $$\begin{equation}
  S_n \;\mapsto\; S_n
  + \delta \cdot \mathrm{sign}\!\left(g_n^S\right)
    \bigl\|g_n^S\bigr\|_1^{q-1}
    \Upsilon^{1-q},
  \label{eq:fgsm_step}
\end{equation}$$ where
$g_n^S = \nabla_{S_n} l_{\mathrm{DH}}(\theta; S_n)$ is the gradient of
the loss with respect to the $n$-th path, computed by automatic
differentiation through the trained network. This is the analogue of the
Fast Gradient Sign Method (FGSM) of [@goodfellow2014explaining]. Instead
of perturbing a single input pointwise, it perturbs the entire empirical
distribution in the direction of steepest ascent of the worst-case
expected loss.

There are two important properties of $\Upsilon$. Firstly, $\Upsilon$
depends on the entire training set, not just a single sample. So, the
attack must compute gradients simultaneously across all $N$ paths and
normalise by $\Upsilon$ at each iteration. Secondly, $\Upsilon$ is
accessible via a single backward pass through the network because is
computed from first-order information (gradients of the loss with
respect to the inputs). This makes the attack computationally tractable.

## Algorithms {#sec:attack_algorithms}

As in [@he2025], now we show how to implement
[\[eq:fgsm_step\]](#eq:fgsm_step){reference-type="eqref"
reference="eq:fgsm_step"} into iterative PGD-like algorithms. This
mimics the methodology in [@he2025].

### Wasserstein Projection Gradient Descent (WPGD) {#sec:wpgd}

In the WPGD algorithm we iteratively apply a small perturbation of size
$\beta$ and then project the perturbed paths onto the Wasserstein ball
of radius $\delta$. We start from $\hat{S}_n = S_n$ then proceed as
follows.

##### Gradient and sensitivity computation.

We compute the per-path gradient
$g_n^S = \nabla_{\hat{S}_n} l_{\mathrm{DH}}(\theta; \hat{S}_n)$ via
backpropagation through the network for $n = 1, \ldots, N$. Compute the
current sensitivity $$\hat{\Upsilon}
  = \left(\frac{1}{N}\sum_{n=1}^N \bigl\|g_n^S\bigr\|_1^q\right)^{1/q}.$$

##### Perturbation update.

We update each perturbed path by a step in the direction of the loss
gradient: $$\begin{equation}
  \hat{S}_n \;\leftarrow\;
  \hat{S}_n + \beta \cdot \mathrm{sign}(g_n^S)
              \cdot \|g_n^S\|_1^{q-1}
              \cdot \hat{\Upsilon}^{1-q}.
  \label{eq:wpgd_update}
\end{equation}$$

##### Projection onto the Wasserstein ball.

As we would like to maintain the constraint
[\[eq:attack_prob\]](#eq:attack_prob){reference-type="eqref"
reference="eq:attack_prob"}, the perturbed paths are projected onto the
Wasserstein ball as such:

$$\begin{equation}
  \hat{S}_n \;\leftarrow\;
  S_n + \max\!\left(1,\, \frac{\delta}{\mathrm{dist}}\right)(\hat{S}_n - S_n),
  \label{eq:wpgd_proj}
\end{equation}$$ where
$\mathrm{dist} = \bigl(\frac{1}{N}\sum_{n=1}^N d(S_n, \hat{S}_n)^p\bigr)^{1/p}$
is the current empirical Wasserstein radius. If
$\mathrm{dist} \leq \delta$ the projection is the identity. Otherwise,
it rescales each perturbation inward until the constraint is exactly
met.

Summarised in the following
Algorithm [\[alg:wpgd\]](#alg:wpgd){reference-type="ref"
reference="alg:wpgd"}.

::: algorithmic
Compute $\hat{\Upsilon} :=
         \bigl(\tfrac{1}{N}\sum_{n=1}^N
         \|\nabla_x l_{\mathrm{DH}}(\theta;\hat{S}_n)\|_1^q\bigr)^{1/q}$
$\hat{S}_n \leftarrow \hat{S}_n
           + \beta\cdot\mathrm{sign}(\nabla_x l_{\mathrm{DH}}(\theta;\hat{S}_n))
           \cdot\|\nabla_x l(\theta;\hat{S}_n)\|_1^{q-1}
           \cdot\hat{\Upsilon}^{1-q}$

### Wasserstein Budget Projection Gradient Descent (WBPGD) {#sec:wbpgd}

The main idea behind the WBPGD algorithm is to separate the magnitude
and direction of the path's perturbation. The key insight is that the
constraint in
[\[eq:attack_prob\]](#eq:attack_prob){reference-type="eqref"
reference="eq:attack_prob"} can be seen as a budget allocation problem.
$d(S_n, \hat{S}_n)^p$ is the budget allocated to the path $S_n$ and the
total must not exceed $\delta^p$.

##### One-step update from the sensitivity result.

The following lemma, which is proved in [@he2025], establishes the
feasibility of this representation by linking it to
[\[eq:fgsm_step\]](#eq:fgsm_step){reference-type="eqref"
reference="eq:fgsm_step"}.

::: lemma
**Lemma 5.1** ([@he2025], Lemma 4.1). *For the one-step
perturbation [\[eq:fgsm_step\]](#eq:fgsm_step){reference-type="eqref"
reference="eq:fgsm_step"}, the budget and direction satisfy
$$\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{budget}_n = \delta \cdot (g_n^b)^{q-1}\Upsilon^{1-q},
  \qquad
  \mathrm{direction}_n = \mathrm{sign}(g_n^d),
  \label{eq:wbpgd_onestep}
\end{equation}$$ where $g_n^b$ and $g_n^d$ are the gradients of
$l_{\mathrm{DH}}(\theta; S_n + b \times d)$ with respect to $b$ and $d$
evaluated at $b = 0$ and
$d = \mathrm{sign}(\nabla_S l_{\mathrm{DH}}(\theta; S_n))$, and
$\Upsilon = \bigl(\frac{1}{N}\sum_{n=1}^N (g_n^b)^q\bigr)^{1/q}$.*
:::

The full procedure is summarised in
Algorithm [\[alg:wbpgd\]](#alg:wbpgd){reference-type="ref"
reference="alg:wbpgd"}.

:::: algorithm
::: algorithmic

Compute $\hat{\Upsilon} :=
         \bigl(\tfrac{1}{N}\sum_{n=1}^N (g_n^b)^q\bigr)^{1/p}$
$\mathrm{budget}_n \leftarrow
           \mathrm{budget}_n + \beta \cdot (g_n^b)^{q-1}\hat{\Upsilon}^{1-q}$
$\mathrm{direction}_n \leftarrow
           \mathrm{direction}_n + (\beta/\delta)\cdot\mathrm{sign}(g_n^d)$
Clamp $\mathrm{direction}_n$ to $[-1,1]$
$\hat{S}_n \leftarrow S_n + \mathrm{budget}_n \times \mathrm{direction}_n$
$\hat{S}_1,\ldots,\hat{S}_N \leftarrow
         \mathrm{Proj}_{\hat{\mathcal{B}}_\delta(\mu)}(\hat{S}_1,\ldots,\hat{S}_N)$
:::
::::

##### Comparison of WPGD and WBPGD.

The algorithms' procedure is different but fundamentally they solve the
same problem,
[\[eq:attack_prob\]](#eq:attack_prob){reference-type="eqref"
reference="eq:attack_prob"}. WPGD attacks the paths directly while WBPGD
optimises the budget and direction separately. Thus, WBPGD can
concentrate its budget on the most \"damageable\" paths. This added
flexibility is translated in higher efficiency, as [@he2025] finds
empirically that WBPGD consistently produces larger CVaR degradation
than WPGD at equal $\delta$, particularly at large perturbation budgets.

### Extension to the Heston Model {#sec:heston_attack}

We have explained previously that the Heston model has two input series.
These are the price process $S^{(1)}$ and the variance process $V$.
During the attack phase, the adversary may perturb one or both series:

- **S-Attack**: only the price trajectory $S^{(1)}$ is perturbed; $V$ is
  held fixed. Algorithms [\[alg:wpgd\]](#alg:wpgd){reference-type="ref"
  reference="alg:wpgd"}
  and [\[alg:wbpgd\]](#alg:wbpgd){reference-type="ref"
  reference="alg:wbpgd"} apply directly.

- **SV-Attack**: both $S^{(1)}$ and $V$ are perturbed jointly. A new
  distance metric on the joint input $(S^{(1)}, V)$ must be defined.

In [@he2025], equation (4.8) defines a joint distance with $\lambda > 0$
as $$\begin{equation}
  d\bigl((S^{(1)}, V),\,(\hat{S}^{(1)}, \hat{V})\bigr)
  = \Bigl(
      \bigl(\max_t|S_t^{(1)} - \hat{S}_t^{(1)}|\bigr)^p
      + \bigl(\lambda\cdot\max_t|V_t - \hat{V}_t|\bigr)^p
    \Bigr)^{1/p}.
  \label{eq:sv_dist}
\end{equation}$$

Under this distance the sensitivity $\Upsilon$ generalises as follows.

::: {#cor:heston_upsilon .corollary}
**Corollary 5.1** ([@he2025], Corollary 4.2). *Under the joint
distance [\[eq:sv_dist\]](#eq:sv_dist){reference-type="eqref"
reference="eq:sv_dist"}, the model sensitivity is $$\begin{equation}
  \Upsilon
  = \left(\sum_{n=1}^N
      \bigl\|\nabla_S l(\theta; S_n, V_n)\bigr\|_1^q
    + \bigl\|{\textstyle\frac{1}{\lambda}}\nabla_V l(\theta; S_n, V_n)\bigr\|_1^q
    \right)^{1/q},
  \label{eq:upsilon_heston}
\end{equation}$$ and the optimal perturbation can be written as the
independent updates $$\begin{align}
  S^{(1)} &\;\mapsto\;
  S^{(1)} + \mathrm{sign}(\nabla_S l(\theta;S,V))
            \cdot\|\nabla_x l(\theta;S,V)\|_*^{q-1}
            \cdot\Upsilon^{1-q},
  \label{eq:sv_update_s}
  \\
  V &\;\mapsto\;
  V + \tfrac{1}{\lambda}\cdot\mathrm{sign}(\nabla_V l(\theta;S,V))
      \cdot\|{\textstyle\frac{1}{\lambda}}\nabla_V l(\theta;S,V)\|_*^{q-1}
      \cdot\Upsilon^{1-q}.
  \label{eq:sv_update_v}
\end{align}$$*
:::

In practice, what
Corollary [5.1](#cor:heston_upsilon){reference-type="ref"
reference="cor:heston_upsilon"} says is that perturbing the joint tuple
$(S^{(1)}, V)$ under the
metric [\[eq:sv_dist\]](#eq:sv_dist){reference-type="eqref"
reference="eq:sv_dist"} is equivalent to independently perturbing the
price series $S^{(1)}$ and the rescaled variance series $\lambda V$
using the $L^\infty$ distance. So, we can treat $S^{(1)}$ and
$\lambda V$ as separate input sequences. Thus, we can apply
Algorithms [\[alg:wpgd\]](#alg:wpgd){reference-type="ref"
reference="alg:wpgd"}
and [\[alg:wbpgd\]](#alg:wbpgd){reference-type="ref"
reference="alg:wbpgd"} directly to the augmented two-column input. As in
[@he2025], we set $\lambda = 1$.

## Adversarial Training {#sec:adv_training}

In this section we implement adversarial training as a remedy to the
distributional adversarial attacks of
Section [5.4](#sec:attack_results){reference-type="ref"
reference="sec:attack_results"}. Instead of training the networks only
on training paths from distribution $\mu$, we interleave the training
with adversarially perturbed paths. This way, the network learns robust
strategies. This directly implements the W-DRO objective introduced in
Section [2.7](#sec:background_dro){reference-type="ref"
reference="sec:background_dro"}.

### Training Objective {#sec:adv_loss}

The adversarial training loss, following [@he2025], is
$$\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{adv}}(\theta)
  = \alpha \sum_{n=1}^N l_{\mathrm{DH}}(\theta; X_n)
  + \sum_{n=1}^N l_{\mathrm{DH}}(\theta; \hat{X}_n),
  \label{eq:adv_loss}
\end{equation}$$ The equation above can be seen as a weighted average
between the standard deep hedging network loss and a worst case term, as
$\{\hat{X}_n\}_{n=1}^N$ are the adversarially perturbed paths. We set
$\alpha = 1$, treating clean and adversarial loss equally, following the
hyperparameter selection of [@he2025]. When $\alpha = 0$ training is
purely adversarial. When $\alpha \to \infty$ it reduces to standard deep
hedging.

The training proceeds as a minimax loop. Iterations alternate. Firstly,
$\theta$ are fixed and the inner attack generates $\{\hat{X}_n\}$. We
use WBPGD with $\delta = 0.1$ and 5 iterations per outer step as
[@he2025]. Then, $\theta$ is updated by a gradient step on
$\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{adv}}(\theta)$. The initial capital $p_0$ does not
affect the optimisation, by cash invariance. Then it is set to zero
[@he2025].

### Implementation Details {#sec:adv_impl}

##### Model and network.

The model and network architecture are the same as described in
Section [4.3](#sec:heston_sim){reference-type="ref"
reference="sec:heston_sim"} and
Section [4.4](#sec:heston_arch){reference-type="ref"
reference="sec:heston_arch"} respectively. The loss function is
$\mathrm{CVaR}_{0.95}$ via the Rockafellar--Uryasev dual form
[@rockafellar2000cvar]

##### Two-stage training.

We adopt a two-stage procedure. In the first stage we train the network
on nominal paths as in Chapters [3](#ch:bs){reference-type="ref"
reference="ch:bs"}, [4](#ch:heston){reference-type="ref"
reference="ch:heston"}. This allows us to initialise the network to a
vanilla hedger before the perturbations are introduced. Subsequently,
the training switches the loss function and uses
[\[eq:adv_loss\]](#eq:adv_loss){reference-type="eqref"
reference="eq:adv_loss"} with a reduced initial learning rate.

##### Attack configuration.

Both the S-attack and SV-Attack are used during training.

##### Dataset and partitioning.

Similarly as to what was done in [@he2025], we would like to understand
the performance of adversarial trained networks compared to vanilla deep
hedging networks. So we generate a total of $100{,}000$ Heston training
paths and partition them into blocks of size
$N \in \{5{,}000;\; 10{,}000;\; 20{,}000;\; 50{,}000;\; 100{,}000\}$.
Then, we use the datasets of each sample size to test the networks and
get mean performance and min-max intervals.

##### Out-of-distribution evaluation.

To asses out-of-distribution performance we generate 50 new Heston
parameter configurations by independently scaling each of
$(\kappa, \theta_{\mathrm{LR}}, \xi, \rho)$ by a factor drawn uniformly
from $[0.9, 1.1]$, introducing bounded deviations of $\pm 10\%$ from the
training parameters. In each case, $10{,}000$ sample paths are
simulated, which then results in as OOD dataset of $500{,}000$ paths.

##### Evaluation metric.

The reported metric is computed from the raw hedging errors:
$$X_n = C_T - \mathrm{PnL}(\theta, \omega_n),$$ where
$C_T = (S_T^{(1)} - K)^+$ is the option payoff and $\mathrm{PnL}$ is the
self-financing trading gain.

Three networks are tested, the clean baseline, the S-Attack

**Adversarial training dominates clean training at small $N$.** The gap

performance than the SV-Attack on small $N$. On the other hand, as the
SV-Attack perturbs both assets, then it is more robust at bigger $N$.

**Lower seed-to-seed variance under adversarial training.** The shaded
min--max bands for both adversarial methods are consistently narrower
than for the clean baseline, especially at small $N$. Therefore, we can
infer that the adversarial training methods are more stable, which is an
important practical property for a risk manager who cannot control which
historical period is used for training.

### Discussion {#sec:adv_discussion}

With the results above,
ections [5.5.3](#sec:adv_oos){reference-type="ref"
reference="sec:adv_oos"} and [5.5.4](#sec:adv_ood){reference-type="ref"
reference="sec:adv_ood"}, we can confirm the results in [@he2025]. In
fact, adversarial training via the W-DRO
loss [\[eq:adv_loss\]](#eq:adv_loss){reference-type="eqref"
reference="eq:adv_loss"} substantially improves both out-of-sample and
out-of-distribution performance of deep hedging networks.

We note that, as each outer parameter update requires an additional
5-step inner attack, the computational cost of adversarial training is
roughly ten times that of clean training for the same epoch budget. This
is the main practical barrier to scaling adversarial training to larger
networks or longer maturities.

# Limitations  {#ch:limitations}

## Limitations of the original Deep Hedging framework {#sec:limitations_buehler}

##### Discrete-time approximation.

The original Deep Hedging framework is formulated in discrete time. The
trading decisions are taken at a finite set of rebalancing dates. Thus,
this produces a finite probability space which is just an approximation
of the continuous time hedging problem. This limitation is carried out
in this dissertation as we have adopted the same methodology.

##### Model and scenario dependence.

The hedging strategy learned by the network is not free from modelling
assumptions even if the paper emphasises its model independence. The
trainer has to choose a scenario generator, a loss function, the hedging
instruments, and the relevant market frictions. So, the hedging strategy
is conditioned on the simulated environment used during training. The
choice of scenario geenration is addressed in [@he2025] and in this
dissertation, which try to improve the robustenss of the network towards
distributional shifts.

##### Dependence on network specification and training procedure.

The hedging policy is directly tied to the network's architecture. It
has been demonstrated by [@buehler2019deep] that efficient training and
convergence can be obtained with a recurrent network and Adam-based
optimisation, but it does not develop a general theory describing how
sensitive the resulting hedge is to architectural or hyperparameter
changes.

##### Scope of the scalability evidence.

The paper does limited research of the higher dimensional problem. In
this dissertation we adapt the methodology to a new model (Bates' model)
but this is far from a systematic study too. The results are encouraging
and could lead to scalability but they do not provide a full complexity
analysis.

##### Summary.

The paper established deep hedging as a frameworkd for discrete time
hedging, but the conclusions are model specific and are not immune to
model misspecification, where the data generated for training might be
different from the one in testing.

### Limitations addressed by [@he2025] and remaining limitations

##### Reduction of model-misspecification sensitivity.

He et al. specifically address this issue. In the original Deep Hedging
framework the data generating distribution during training could be
different from the test distribution under which the trained networks
performs the final hedging. They address this issue by formulating deep
hedging as a Wasserstein distributionally robust optimisation problem.
They construct dversarial perturbations of the input distribution. In
this way they directly tackle this limitation.

##### Local nature of the robustification.

One limitation of this approach is that the training procedure is based
on a first-order sensitivity approximation of the Wasserstein DRO
objective as the ambiguity radius $\delta \downarrow 0$. Therefore, as
the approximation is justified by a small Wasserstein radii, the paper
just improves robustness in a neighbourhood of the reference
distribution, rather than providing an exact solution to the full inner
maximisation problem for arbitrary perturbation size.

Probably a mroe relevant robustness test would involve training on one
model and evaluation on a structurally different one, or a mix of
models. In this case, the distribution shift is both larger and more
realistic. Also, the model is only tested on European Calls.

##### Dependence on the Wasserstein radius.

The authors specify that the performance of the networks remain
sensitive to the choice of the Wasserstein radius [@he2025]. This may be
different between models. This means that the method requires an
additional calibration step, since $\delta$ controls the strength of the
adversarial perturbation.

##### Computational cost of adversarial training.

As we have seen in the Methodology section above, the adversarial
training is substantially more expensive than the classical deep hedging
training as there is a need for an inner optimisation step to generate
the adversarial perturbation. This is approximated to about 10 times
more expensive. Therefore, it is important to understand the
cost-benefit of this methodology.

##### Scope of the numerical evidence.

Numerical evidence is provided in [@he2025] on a finite collection of
benchmarks. No general theorem or result is proven. This dissertation
tested the methodology on Bates model but has the same limitation. The
paper's conclusions should accordingly be read as numerical evidence for
robustness in representative examples, rather than as a complete
characterisation of performance across all possible financial
environments.

### Limited Network Capacity {#sec:network_capacity}

The networks employed in [@he2025] are quite narrow, as they have two
hidden layers of width 20, totalling roughly 500 trainable parameters
per time step. This is sufficient if we look at GBM or Heston models,
which have low dimensional inputs. It may be inadequate for richer
information sets.

# Conclusions {#ch:conclusions}

This dissertation has focused on replicating the results in
[@buehler2019deep] and [@he2025] and extneding it to \...

With regards to the results in [@buehler2019deep] we managed to
replicate them both for the GBM and Heston model. We can confirm that a
neural network trained with CVaR loss with a feed-forward architecture
recovers the respective theoretical deltas when the risk-aversion
parameter is $\alpha=0.50$. When $\alpha$ increases the networks
sacrifices the mean loss in order to severely damp the left tail. These
results faithfully reproduce the primary empirical findings of
[@buehler2019deep] and confirm both the correctness of our
implementation and our understanding of the OCE framework introduced in
the Background chapter. In the Heston model implementation we confirm
that these networks can work with two assets. Also, we conclude that a
stock-only hedge fails to control tail losses under stochastic
volatility.

In the second part of this dissertation, the study confirms that
classically trained networks are substantially vulnerable to
distributional perturbations. The realised $\mathrm{CVaR}_{0.99}$
roughly doubles at $\delta=0.1$ and quadruples at $\delta=0.5$. This
vulnerability is greater in absolute terms for networks with a higher
$\alpha$. This is a paradox as the training criterion that best protects
against tail losses under the nominal distribution makes the network
most exploitable by adversarial distributional shifts. This is why
[@he2025] introduced adversarial trained networks. Results show that
adversarially trained networks have a better out-of-sample and
out-of-distribution performance. These findings are fully consistent
with [@he2025] and confirm that the W-DRO adversarial attack framework
is a theoretically well-founded and computationally tractable.
